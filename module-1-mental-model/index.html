How AI Actually Thinks

Get the working model that turns AI from a slot machine into a Chief of Staff.

20 min read · The mental model under everything else
The Frame

Congrats — you just hired AI.

The whole accelerator rests on one frame. Get this right and the rest of the program clicks into place. Get this wrong and every prompting trick you learn lands as a hack instead of a discipline.

Imagine you just hired a brilliant Chief of Staff. They are the smartest, most capable person you've ever seen at research, execution, automation, and reasoning. The only problem? They're also a dumb Millennial. They know nothing about what you actually want, what matters to you, where things are to reference, and they need constant validation and feedback.

Congrats. You just hired AI.

That's the whole mental model. Every framework in the program — prompting, projects, context, delegation — is a different answer to the same question: how do you make a brilliant-but-blank Chief of Staff useful?

The work isn't learning AI. The work is becoming the kind of principal a brilliant new hire can actually be useful to.

What's Inside The Box

Pattern, not knowledge. That's the whole machine.

The model does one thing well. It predicts what comes next, given everything it has already read. Trained on a vast slice of human writing, it has learned the shape of how ideas, sentences, and arguments fit together.

When you prompt it, you are not querying a database. You are handing the model a starting context and asking it to extend the pattern. The output is fluent because the patterns are dense. The output is sometimes wrong because patterns of writing and patterns of truth are not the same thing.

The quality of what you get back is downstream of the context you put in. Garbage context, generic output. Sharp context, sharp output. The model is not the bottleneck. You are.

Two practical implications follow. First, the model has no memory of you unless you give it one. Every chat starts at zero. Second, it will confidently invent things to keep the pattern going if you do not constrain it. Both facts shape every skill you will learn next.

The Relationship

Three rings describe how the relationship matures.

Each ring expands what your Chief of Staff does on your behalf. The skills compound. You cannot skip a ring without paying for it later — you cannot delegate what you cannot encode, and you cannot encode what you cannot specify.

01 · Tool

Single tasks. One prompt, one answer.

"Do this one thing for me."

You describe what you want; AI produces information. Most people start and stop here.

Skill installed: prompting · the 4 C's

02 · Collaborator

Multi-step work, judgment shared.

"Help me think this through with me."

Research, analysis, drafting. You encode taste, voice, and process so the AI references them automatically.

+ Context architecture · Projects · Reusable skills

03 · Delegate

Agents act on your behalf, with verification.

"Just handle this. Tell me if something needs me."

Bounded autonomy inside rules you set. You keep the boundary; the system runs. Only works because Ring 2 came first.

+ Connections · Scheduled tasks · Agent design

Each ring is a transfer of a different kind of judgment. Ring 1, you transfer the task and keep all judgment. Ring 2, you transfer process and taste, and keep the decision. Ring 3, you transfer decision authority within bounds, and keep verification.

You cannot delegate what you cannot encode. You cannot encode what you cannot specify. The rings build on each other for a reason.
The Executive Shift

Four habits that come straight from the frame.

Most executives treat AI like a search box, then complain when the output is generic. The Chief of Staff frame fixes that without teaching you a single prompt trick. Drop these expectations and the quality of your AI output changes the same day.

  1. Stop expecting the model to know you
    It doesn't. Every chat starts at zero unless you supply context. That isn't a limitation to work around. It's the design. Brief it the way you would a new hire on day one.
  2. Stop expecting the model to push back
    It defaults to agreement and validation. If you want a real read, ask for it explicitly. "Find three weak spots in this." "Steelman the opposite." "Don't tell me this is good unless it actually is."
  3. Stop expecting one prompt to do the job
    The first response is a draft, not an answer. Stay in the conversation. "Closer. The part that's off is X. Fix that part." Refining beats restarting.
  4. Start treating context as the asset
    Every minute you spend giving context, the AI repays in output quality. The execs who win are the ones who get good at briefing. Everything else in the program is just a structured way to do this well.
Locate Yourself

Three questions that don't lie.

Most execs over-rate themselves by one ring. Show, don't tell. Try to answer each of these from your own work this week — the artifacts reveal where you actually are.

Ring 1 · Tool

Show me a prompt you wrote this week.

If you can — you're operating at Tool.

Ring 2 · Collaborator

Show me a skill or saved instruction you reuse.

If you can — you're operating at Collaborator.

Ring 3 · Delegate

Show me something that runs without you.

If you can — you're operating at Delegate.

The target isn't to push everything to Ring 3. It's to know which ring each piece of your work belongs in, and to do that ring well. A mature exec operator has a handful of Ring 1 prompts they fire daily, a working library and five to fifteen Ring 2 skills they reuse, and one to five Ring 3 workflows running with verification.
